#+title:    Recipe parser test plan
#+language: en

* Purpose

The paste-a-recipe flow feeds a heuristic parser: the first non-empty line
becomes the name, ingredient lines are reduced to bare food names for the
recipe's chips, and the paste is kept verbatim as the instructions.  This plan
tests that flow against the shapes recipes actually take in the wild, and
against the catalog-matching behaviour the chips depend on — a chip only
inherits its tier tint, stock tracking, and shopping-list identity when its name
matches a Longevity Foods entry, so a parse that emits "Tomato" where the
catalog says "Tomatoes" quietly disconnects the ingredient from the rest of the
app and, once sent to the Shopping List, duplicates a food the kitchen already
tracks under another name.

The plan has two halves: a fidelity suite (does the parse mirror the recipe?)
and a canonicalization suite (do parsed names resolve to catalog names?).  The
canonicalization behaviour does not exist yet; its tests are written first and
drive a small parser extension.

* How the tests run

A new Elm unit-test suite exercises the parser directly — the parser is a pure
function, so the whole matrix runs as fast unit tests with no browser or server
involved.

- ~elm-explorations/test~ joins the frontend's test dependencies, and the
  ~elm-test~ runner joins the Nix dev shell.
- The suite lives beside the frontend sources in a ~tests~ directory, one module
  for parsing fidelity and one for canonicalization.
- ~just test~ grows an ~elm-test~ step so the suite gates every change the same
  way the Rust suites do.
- Each real-world recipe is captured as a fixture string in the test module,
  pasted-text-shaped, with a comment naming where it came from.

A closing manual pass drives the real app once (paste, inspect chips, export) to
confirm the unit-level behaviour survives the UI plumbing.

* The corpus: real recipes, varied formats

During execution I will fetch recipes from the live web and reduce each to the
text a user would realistically select and paste.  The corpus is chosen for
format diversity, not cuisine:

1. A big recipe-site format (AllRecipes-style): "Ingredients" and "Directions"
   headers, decimal and fraction quantities, servings and nutrition lines mixed
   in.
2. A newspaper format (NYT Cooking-style): yield/time preamble, unicode
   fractions (½, ¼), "Step 1 / Step 2" instruction labels.
3. A long-preamble food blog (Smitten Kitchen / Budget Bytes-style): paragraphs
   of story before the recipe card, section headers inside the ingredient list
   ("For the sauce:"), notes after the steps.
4. A British format (BBC Good Food-style): metric units (g, ml), "Method"
   instead of "Instructions", terse step paragraphs.
5. A baking format (King Arthur-style): weights alongside volumes ("120g (1
   cup)"), "divided" ingredients, temperature-heavy steps.
6. A minimal hand-typed format: no headers at all, just a title, dashed
   ingredient bullets, and free-text steps — the shape a user types from memory.
7. An email/markdown format: asterisk bullets, numbered steps as "1)" and "1.",
   hard-wrapped lines mid-sentence.
8. The app's own export format: a recipe exported to text and pasted back
   (round-trip: the re-parse should reproduce the same name, the same chips, and
   instructions containing the same lines).

* Fidelity suite

For every corpus fixture, assertions cover the three parse products:

** Name

- The recipe's actual title is chosen, not a yield line, a rating, or a URL that
  happens to precede it in the selection.
- Titles longer than the app's name cap are truncated by the caller; the
  parser's output is asserted pre-cap.

** Ingredients

- Every real ingredient in the fixture yields exactly one chip; no chip appears
  for section headers, servings lines, or nutrition rows.
- Quantity forms are stripped wherever they lead a line: integers, decimals,
  ranges ("2-3", "2 to 3"), unicode and ascii fractions ("½", "1/2"),
  multipliers ("2 x 400g"), and measure words from cups through cloves to
  fillets.
- Prep words are stripped when leading ("finely chopped onion") and cut when
  trailing after a comma ("onion, finely chopped").
- Parentheticals are cut ("(15 oz)", "(optional)"), as is everything after the
  first comma.
- "To taste", "for serving", "divided", and "or"-alternatives ("cilantro or
  parsley") are exercised and their current behaviour pinned down; where current
  behaviour is unsatisfying, the case is marked as driving a parser fix rather
  than silently accepted.
- Duplicates within one recipe collapse case-insensitively, keeping first
  occurrence and its casing.

** Instructions

- The verbatim-keep contract holds: every step line of the fixture appears in
  the instructions, amounts intact, in order.
- Numbered ("1.", "1)", "Step 1") and unnumbered step styles survive.
- Windows line endings and hard-wrapped paragraphs do not corrupt the text.

* Canonicalization suite

The new behaviour under test: parsed ingredient names resolve against the food
catalog so chips connect to the pyramid instead of duplicating it.

** Matching rules

Resolution tries, in order, and otherwise leaves the parsed name as is:

1. Exact match, case-insensitive: "tomatoes" adopts the catalog casing
   "Tomatoes".
2. Whole-name plural fold, both directions: "tomato" resolves to "Tomatoes";
   "chickpea" to "Chickpeas"; "berry" to "Berries" (s / es / ies-y endings
   only).

Substring and fuzzy matching are deliberately excluded: "coconut milk" must
never collapse into "Coconut", nor "olive oil" into "Olives".  Multi-word names
fold only on their last word ("sweet potato" resolves to "Sweet potato" exactly;
"sweet potatoes" folds to it).

** Cases

- Singular in the paste, plural in the catalog: tomato → Tomatoes; carrot →
  Carrots; date → Dates.
- Plural in the paste, singular in the catalog: avocados → Avocado; sweet
  potatoes → Sweet potato; lemons → Lemons (already plural).
- Casing only: EXTRA-VIRGIN OLIVE OIL and extra-virgin olive oil both adopt
  "Extra-virgin olive oil".
- ies/y folds: cherries → Cherries (exact), berry → Berries.
- Must-not-merge pairs, asserted to stay distinct: Coconut milk vs Coconut;
  Olive oil vs Olives; Date syrup vs Dates; Green onions vs Onions; Red lentils
  vs Lentils.
- Unknown foods pass through untouched, capitalized, and become ordinary
  unlinked chips ("Saffron threads" when only "Saffron" exists is pinned down
  explicitly — the last-word fold must not force it to "Saffron").
- The within-recipe dedup runs after canonicalization, so "tomato" and
  "Tomatoes" in one paste produce a single chip.

** Where canonicalization applies

The parser gains the catalog's names as an argument at its call site (the paste
flow already has the loaded model in hand).  The AI-recipe and typed-ingredient
flows are out of scope here but are natural follow-ups; the plan notes them so
the decision is deliberate.

* Robustness suite

- Empty paste and whitespace-only paste (the caller already guards; the parser's
  own behaviour is still pinned).
- A paste with no recognizable structure at all (a news article): the parse must
  not invent ingredients from prose.
- Very long pastes (a full blog page with comments) parse in reasonable time and
  cap nothing unexpectedly.
- Emoji, smart quotes, and accented ingredients (jalapeño, açaí) survive intact
  and match their catalog spellings.
- Ingredient lines using unusual bullets (–, —, ·, •) and tab indentation are
  recognized.
- A paste whose "Ingredients" header repeats (two recipes in one paste) is
  pinned to current behaviour and documented.

* Acceptance criteria

- Every fixture's assertions pass, or the failing case is either fixed in the
  parser within this work or explicitly recorded as a known limitation in the
  test with a comment saying why.
- The canonicalization suite passes in full — it is the new behaviour and ships
  with this work.
- The must-not-merge list passes in full; a false merge is a defect, not a
  limitation.
- ~just test~ runs the Elm suite and fails the build on any regression.
- The manual UI pass confirms: pasted chips carry tier tints for catalog foods,
  the cart flow adds the catalog-named item, and the export/paste round-trip is
  stable.

* Execution order

1. Wire the harness: test dependencies, dev shell runner, ~just test~.
2. Write the canonicalization suite (red), implement resolution in the parser,
   drive to green.
3. Fetch the corpus, reduce each recipe to paste-shaped fixtures, write the
   fidelity suite; fix the parser where a case warrants it, or record the
   limitation.
4. Robustness suite.
5. Manual UI pass, then the changelog entry and the usual review.
