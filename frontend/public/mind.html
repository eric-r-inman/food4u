The MIND Diet

Eating for the brain

MIND — the Mediterranean-DASH Intervention for Neurodegenerative Delay — was developed in 2015 by Martha Clare Morris and colleagues at Rush University Medical Center. It blends the two best-studied healthy-eating patterns, Mediterranean and DASH, and keeps the food groups most strongly tied to brain health, with simple weekly targets for each.

In the original cohort studies, close adherence was associated with markedly slower cognitive decline and lower Alzheimer's risk — and even moderate adherence showed benefit, which is unusual among dietary patterns. Follow-up trials are still working out how much is cause and how much is correlation, but the pattern itself is a sensible, food-first way to eat by any standard.

The food lists

Eat Ten brain-healthy groups
  • Green leafy vegetables 6+ servings / week
  • Other vegetables 1+ serving / day
  • Berries 2+ servings / week
  • Nuts 5+ servings / week
  • Beans & legumes 4+ meals / week
  • Whole grains 3+ servings / day
  • Fish, omega-3-rich 1+ meal / week
  • Poultry 2+ meals / week
  • Extra-virgin olive oil principal cooking fat
  • Wine, optional ≤ 1 glass / day, or none
Limit Five scored ceilings
  • Butter & stick margarine < 1 Tbsp / day
  • Cheese ≤ 1 serving / week
  • Red meat ≤ 3 meals / week
  • Fried & fast food < 1 serving / week
  • Pastries & sweets ≤ 4 servings / week
Avoid Rare exceptions, not habits
  • Partially hydrogenated oils trans fats
  • Sugar-sweetened drinks sodas, sweet teas
  • Processed meats bacon, hot dogs, deli
  • Deep-fried snacks chips, doughnuts
  • Ultra-processed foods packaged snack aisle

The Eat and Limit targets are the food groups the MIND studies scored; the Avoid column is what those studies effectively scored at zero. In the app, the Staples Tracker's Auto menu stocks the MIND pantry with one press, and the Recipes column filters by the MIND tag.
