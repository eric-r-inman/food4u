#+title:     Changelog
#+language:  en

* Upcoming
** Added
1. On a narrow screen, where the columns stack instead of sitting side by side,
   a collapsed column now lies flat as a full-width bar with its title reading
   horizontally; the sideways title is only for the side-by-side layout.
2. The server ships as a single self-contained binary: the compiled frontend is
   embedded into it at build time, so a release needs no asset directory
   alongside — and in development the assets are still read live from disk.
3. Arrange the columns however you like: drag a column by its title bar (or its
   collapsed bar) and drop it on another column to take that side of it — a
   vertical line previews where it will land. The arrangement is saved with your
   kitchen, so it follows your account.
4. An About page describes the app, its philosophy, and each column, linked from
   the toolbar next to the title.
5. Breakfast, Lunch, and Dinner are meal tags now, not recipe categories: the
   recipe box organizes by dish type alone, and the tag filter slices by meal
   instead. Recipes previously filed under a meal re-home themselves to a dish
   category with their meal as a tag. The bundled defaults also gain six
   pressure-cooker and slow-cooker dishes, tagged "Instant Pot" and "Slow
   Cooker".
6. An expanded recipe card shows its full name on a line of its own beneath the
   header row, and recipe names are capped at 48 characters so that line always
   fits.
7. Plan the days ahead in a new Meal Planner column between Recipes and Kitchen:
   a pane per day ("Day 1" onward — add or remove days, up to ten), each with
   Breakfast, Lunch, Dinner, and Snacks slots. Drag a recipe card into a slot to
   plan it — the recipe is copied by reference, so it stays in the Recipes
   column and the same recipe can be planned any number of times.  A planned
   entry is a full-width card: its magnifying glass jumps to the recipe in the
   Recipes column and scrolls it into view, and its ✕ unplans it. Days collapse
   like panes, and the whole column collapses like its neighbours.
8. The bundled defaults now open onto a stocked recipe collection: six recipes
   in every category, drawn from Mediterranean, Blue Zone, MIND,
   anti-inflammatory, and DASH cooking, each tagged by its diet plan, by
   convenience (quick, one-pot, no-cook), and by any featured fish, poultry, or
   eggs — along with the few pantry foods they needed added to the Longevity
   Foods pyramid.
9. Edit the bundled default foods and recipes visually: ~just seed-edit~ opens
   the app on a throwaway copy of the defaults, and ~just seed-save~ bakes what
   you changed back into the shipped seed.
10. Export a recipe to a plain-text file from the ⬇ button in its header; the
    file is named after the recipe and lists its tags, ingredients, and
    instructions.
11. Tag recipes with your own labels and filter by one: tags are edited as chips
    in the expanded recipe card, and a dropdown beside "Search recipes" narrows
    the list to recipes carrying the chosen tag, re-counting each category to
    match.
12. Reorder recipes by dragging: drop a card onto another to slot it in just
    above, or onto a category to send it to that category's end — across
    categories too. The whole card floats with the pointer as you drag it by its
    grip — the same feel as dragging a food badge — a blue line marks where it
    will land, and the card's own category stays open while it drags.
13. Each Longevity Foods tier header shows how many of its foods are in stock
    beside its food count.
14. Bookmark a recipe from its header, between the drag grip and the cart button
    — a marker for remembering a recipe, such as one whose ingredients you have
    just sent to the Shopping List. A recipe category's title row shows how many
    of its recipes are bookmarked. Bookmarked recipes read at a glance as a
    light-blue pill.
15. Select several items and move them together: a "Select: off / on" toggle in
    its own row under the page title turns on a tap-to-select circle on every
    item badge. Move the whole selection either by dragging one selected badge,
    or — with no dragging at all, so it works on a phone — by pressing the green
    ⬇ "Move selected here" arrow that appears on each Kitchen pane, Longevity
    category, and Shopping List category while items are selected; the items
    stay selected so you can send them somewhere else too. While on, the toggle
    counts the current selection — "Select: on (5)". A "Deselect all" button
    appears beside the toggle once more than one item is picked. Badges still
    drag individually with a click-and-hold. The Select row stays pinned on
    screen with the title bar wherever the page itself scrolls.
16. Organize the Shopping List into your own categories: it starts with a
    default set (Produce, Canned & Dry Goods, Dairy, and so on), and you can add
    your own, drag to-buy items into any of them, and delete a category — with a
    confirmation — from the ✕ on its title row.  Each category collapses to a
    labelled row with a count, matching the Recipes column.  Recipe and staple
    additions still land in the main list, ready to sort.  (The automatic
    grocery-department grouping is replaced by these categories.)
17. Delete a Longevity Foods category with the ✕ on its title row, with a
    confirmation.
18. Add your own food category to any tier of the Longevity Foods pyramid, then
    fill it with foods like the built-in categories.
19. Generate recipes with AI: an "AI" button in each recipe category opens an
    assistant that creates a recipe from the meal category, your remembered
    preferences and allergies, and — if you like — what is in your Kitchen; you
    preview it before it is added, and its missing ingredients can go straight
    to the Shopping List.  You bring your own LLM key (Google Gemini's free
    tier, OpenAI/ChatGPT, or Anthropic Claude); the request goes from your
    browser directly to the provider and your key is kept only in your browser.
20. Sign in and sign out through an OIDC identity provider, with each account
    keeping its own kitchen.
21. Add and remove Kitchen storage panes.
22. Edit a Kitchen pane's name and description inline — a pencil opens the
    editor, Enter saves, and Escape cancels without saving.
23. A Staples Tracker pane in the Kitchen: drag in the foods you always want on
    hand, see the ones you are out of turn red, and send those to the Shopping
    List with one button.
24. Move a recipe to another category by dragging it there; categories highlight
    as you drag over them.
25. Seed a new account with the default storage panes on its first sign-in.
26. =just login-demo= runs the app against a local identity provider for trying
    the signed-in experience.
27. Rename the app to Longevity Pantry (the code keeps the =food4u= name).
28. Recolour a Kitchen pane's header from the app's palette while editing it,
    and delete a pane — with a confirmation — from the same editor.
** Fixes
1. A recipe's cart button is now green only when the recipe has an ingredient
   that is neither in the Kitchen nor already on the Shopping List; when there
   is nothing left to add it is greyed out, so a glance says which recipes still
   need shopping.
2. The "Paste" and "AI" buttons in a recipe category now work: pressing one no
   longer dismisses the add row before the click takes effect.
3. Adding items after a fresh page load — by dragging, by the Staples Tracker
   cart button, and so on — no longer occasionally fails to save; newly created
   items are given ids that cannot collide with existing ones.
4. The Staples Tracker now starts collapsed, and its shopping-cart button stands
   out clearly against the header — and turns green when it holds staples not
   yet in the Kitchen or on the Shopping List.
5. Editing a Kitchen pane no longer clips the controls on the right edge, and
   clicking away from the pane closes the editor without saving.
** Breaking
1. The frontend_path setting (config file, CLI flag, and environment variable)
   is gone: the server no longer serves the frontend from a directory, since the
   compiled assets are embedded into the binary.
