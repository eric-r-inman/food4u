* v0.1.0
** Added
1. Sync with the project template (rust-template 65f9706): the standard Publish
   and Release-binaries GitHub workflows are added — inert here, since the
   project ships no release binaries — and the contribution docs gain the
   template's Release-binaries and changelog-gate conventions. The Windows
   cross-build the template now ships is opted out (the server embeds an SQL
   seed the cross helper drops, and a hosted Linux service ships no Windows
   binary), recorded honestly under compliance-ignores. No adopter action.
2. A food's alternate grocery forms are named base-first now — "Salmon, canned",
   "Figs, dried", "Tomatoes, canned, diced" — so an alphabetized Longevity Foods
   list keeps every form beside the food it comes from rather than scattering
   them under "Canned", "Dried", and the like. More canned staples join along
   the way: red, great northern, and white beans, crushed tomatoes, and jarred
   tomato sauce.
3. The Longevity Foods catalog gains the pantry forms of foods it already
   teaches, where those forms are common in longevity cooking: canned tomatoes,
   beans, and mackerel; jarred roasted red peppers and artichoke hearts; whole-
   grain and nut flours (rye, whole-wheat, buckwheat, oat, chickpea, almond,
   cornmeal); dried figs, apricots, cranberries, and shiitake; almond and oat
   milk; applesauce; smoked salmon and mackerel; brown rice flour; and ground
   ginger and garlic powder. Each is its own entry with its own stock tracking,
   beside its fresh counterpart.
4. Stock the Staples Tracker with one press: an "Auto" button under its title
   bar offers the five longevity diets the recipes are tagged with
   (Mediterranean, Blue Zone, MIND, DASH, Anti-Inflammatory), and picking one
   fills the tracker with that diet's essential staples. Staples already present
   are never duplicated, so re-running a diet is harmless and stacking two diets
   yields their union.
5. A Donate page, linked from the toolbar beside About: a placeholder — "Work in
   progress" — while a way to support the app is set up.
6. Clear or export the whole week from the Meal Planner: a toolbar under its
   title bar, matching the Shopping List's, adds an "Export to .txt" button and
   a "Clear all" button that empties every day's slots in one press. The
   exported file lays out the plan day by day, then prints the full text of
   every recipe it draws on, so it is enough to cook the week from on its own.
7. Pasted recipes now read far more of the wild's formats — checkbox and dash
   bullets, "(15-ounce)" can sizes, fused units like "28-ounce", price notes,
   "or"-alternatives, subsection labels, and "juice of 1 lemon" — and every
   parsed ingredient resolves against the Longevity Foods catalog, so "tomato"
   joins the "Tomatoes" your kitchen already tracks instead of arriving as a
   lookalike. Near-misses never merge: coconut milk stays distinct from coconut,
   olive oil from olives.
8. On a narrow screen, where the columns stack instead of sitting side by side,
   a collapsed column now lies flat as a full-width bar with its title reading
   horizontally; the sideways title is only for the side-by-side layout.
9. The server ships as a single self-contained binary: the compiled frontend is
   embedded into it at build time, so a release needs no asset directory
   alongside — and in development the assets are still read live from disk.
10. Arrange the columns however you like: drag a column by its title bar (or its
    collapsed bar) and drop it on another column to take that side of it — a
    vertical line previews where it will land. The arrangement is saved with
    your kitchen, so it follows your account.
11. An About page describes the app, its philosophy, and each column, linked
    from the toolbar next to the title.
12. Breakfast, Lunch, and Dinner are meal tags now, not recipe categories: the
    recipe box organizes by dish type alone, and the tag filter slices by meal
    instead. Recipes previously filed under a meal re-home themselves to a dish
    category with their meal as a tag. The bundled defaults also gain six
    pressure-cooker and slow-cooker dishes, tagged "Instant Pot" and "Slow
    Cooker".
13. An expanded recipe card shows its full name on a line of its own beneath the
    header row, and recipe names are capped at 48 characters so that line always
    fits.
14. Plan the days ahead in a new Meal Planner column between Recipes and
    Kitchen: a pane per day ("Day 1" onward — add or remove days, up to ten),
    each with Breakfast, Lunch, Dinner, and Snacks slots. Drag a recipe card
    into a slot to plan it — the recipe is copied by reference, so it stays in
    the Recipes column and the same recipe can be planned any number of times.
    A planned entry is a full-width card: its magnifying glass jumps to the
    recipe in the Recipes column and scrolls it into view, and its ✕ unplans it.
    Days collapse like panes, and the whole column collapses like its
    neighbours.
15. The bundled defaults now open onto a stocked recipe collection: six recipes
    in every category, drawn from Mediterranean, Blue Zone, MIND,
    anti-inflammatory, and DASH cooking, each tagged by its diet plan, by
    convenience (quick, one-pot, no-cook), and by any featured fish, poultry, or
    eggs — along with the few pantry foods they needed added to the Longevity
    Foods pyramid.
16. Edit the bundled default foods and recipes visually: ~just seed-edit~ opens
    the app on a throwaway copy of the defaults, and ~just seed-save~ bakes what
    you changed back into the shipped seed.
17. Export a recipe to a plain-text file from the ⬇ button in its header; the
    file is named after the recipe and lists its tags, ingredients, and
    instructions.
18. Tag recipes with your own labels and filter by one: tags are edited as chips
    in the expanded recipe card, and a dropdown beside "Search recipes" narrows
    the list to recipes carrying the chosen tag, re-counting each category to
    match.
19. Reorder recipes by dragging: drop a card onto another to slot it in just
    above, or onto a category to send it to that category's end — across
    categories too. The whole card floats with the pointer as you drag it by its
    grip — the same feel as dragging a food badge — a blue line marks where it
    will land, and the card's own category stays open while it drags.
20. Each Longevity Foods tier header shows how many of its foods are in stock
    beside its food count.
21. Bookmark a recipe from its header, between the drag grip and the cart button
    — a marker for remembering a recipe, such as one whose ingredients you have
    just sent to the Shopping List. A recipe category's title row shows how many
    of its recipes are bookmarked. Bookmarked recipes read at a glance as a
    light-blue pill.
22. Select several items and move them together: a "Select: off / on" toggle in
    its own row under the page title turns on a tap-to-select circle on every
    item badge. Move the whole selection either by dragging one selected badge,
    or — with no dragging at all, so it works on a phone — by pressing the green
    ⬇ "Move selected here" arrow that appears on each Kitchen pane, Longevity
    category, and Shopping List category while items are selected; the items
    stay selected so you can send them somewhere else too. While on, the toggle
    counts the current selection — "Select: on (5)". A "Deselect all" button
    appears beside the toggle once more than one item is picked. Badges still
    drag individually with a click-and-hold. The Select row stays pinned on
    screen with the title bar wherever the page itself scrolls.
23. Organize the Shopping List into your own categories: it starts with a
    default set (Produce, Canned & Dry Goods, Dairy, and so on), and you can add
    your own, drag to-buy items into any of them, and delete a category — with a
    confirmation — from the ✕ on its title row.  Each category collapses to a
    labelled row with a count, matching the Recipes column.  Recipe and staple
    additions still land in the main list, ready to sort.  (The automatic
    grocery-department grouping is replaced by these categories.)
24. Delete a Longevity Foods category with the ✕ on its title row, with a
    confirmation.
25. Add your own food category to any tier of the Longevity Foods pyramid, then
    fill it with foods like the built-in categories.
26. Generate recipes with AI: an "AI" button in each recipe category opens an
    assistant that creates a recipe from the meal category, your remembered
    preferences and allergies, and — if you like — what is in your Kitchen; you
    preview it before it is added, and its missing ingredients can go straight
    to the Shopping List.  You bring your own LLM key (Google Gemini's free
    tier, OpenAI/ChatGPT, or Anthropic Claude); the request goes from your
    browser directly to the provider and your key is kept only in your browser.
27. Sign in and sign out through an OIDC identity provider, with each account
    keeping its own kitchen.
28. Add and remove Kitchen storage panes.
29. Edit a Kitchen pane's name and description inline — a pencil opens the
    editor, Enter saves, and Escape cancels without saving.
30. A Staples Tracker pane in the Kitchen: drag in the foods you always want on
    hand, see the ones you are out of turn red, and send those to the Shopping
    List with one button.
31. Move a recipe to another category by dragging it there; categories highlight
    as you drag over them.
32. Seed a new account with the default storage panes on its first sign-in.
33. =just login-demo= runs the app against a local identity provider for trying
    the signed-in experience.
34. Rename the app to Longevity Pantry (the code keeps the =food4u= name).
35. Recolour a Kitchen pane's header from the app's palette while editing it,
    and delete a pane — with a confirmation — from the same editor.
** Fixes
1. The packaged build no longer cross-compiles a Windows binary of the server —
   a hosted Linux service ships none, and that build could not carry the SQL
   seed the server embeds — so the release build and its CI smoke check pass
   again.
2. A recipe's cart button is now green only when the recipe has an ingredient
   that is neither in the Kitchen nor already on the Shopping List; when there
   is nothing left to add it is greyed out, so a glance says which recipes still
   need shopping.
3. The "Paste" and "AI" buttons in a recipe category now work: pressing one no
   longer dismisses the add row before the click takes effect.
4. Adding items after a fresh page load — by dragging, by the Staples Tracker
   cart button, and so on — no longer occasionally fails to save; newly created
   items are given ids that cannot collide with existing ones.
5. The Staples Tracker now starts collapsed, and its shopping-cart button stands
   out clearly against the header — and turns green when it holds staples not
   yet in the Kitchen or on the Shopping List.
6. Editing a Kitchen pane no longer clips the controls on the right edge, and
   clicking away from the pane closes the editor without saving.
** Breaking
1. The frontend_path setting (config file, CLI flag, and environment variable)
   is gone: the server no longer serves the frontend from a directory, since the
   compiled assets are embedded into the binary.
