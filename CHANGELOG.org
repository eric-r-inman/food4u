#+title:     Changelog
#+language:  en

* Upcoming
** Added
1. Plan the week in a new Meal Planner column between Recipes and Kitchen: a
   pane per day, Sunday first, each with Breakfast, Lunch, Dinner, and Snacks
   slots. Drag a recipe card into a slot to plan it — the recipe is copied by
   reference, so it stays in the Recipes column and the same recipe can be
   planned any number of times. A planned entry opens its recipe on click and
   removes with its ✕; days collapse like panes, and the whole column collapses
   like its neighbours.
2. The bundled defaults now open onto a stocked recipe collection: six recipes
   in every category, drawn from Mediterranean, Blue Zone, MIND,
   anti-inflammatory, and DASH cooking, each tagged by its diet plan, by
   convenience (quick, one-pot, no-cook), and by any featured fish, poultry, or
   eggs — along with the few pantry foods they needed added to the Longevity
   Foods pyramid.
3. Edit the bundled default foods and recipes visually: ~just seed-edit~ opens
   the app on a throwaway copy of the defaults, and ~just seed-save~ bakes what
   you changed back into the shipped seed.
4. Export a recipe to a plain-text file from the ⬇ button in its header; the
   file is named after the recipe and lists its tags, ingredients, and
   instructions.
5. Tag recipes with your own labels and filter by one: tags are edited as chips
   in the expanded recipe card, and a dropdown beside "Search recipes" narrows
   the list to recipes carrying the chosen tag, re-counting each category to
   match.
6. Reorder recipes by dragging: drop a card onto another to slot it in just
   above, or onto a category to send it to that category's end — across
   categories too. The whole card floats with the pointer as you drag it by its
   grip — the same feel as dragging a food badge — a blue line marks where it
   will land, and the card's own category stays open while it drags.
7. Each Longevity Foods tier header shows how many of its foods are in stock
   beside its food count.
8. Bookmark a recipe from its header, between the drag grip and the cart button
   — a marker for remembering a recipe, such as one whose ingredients you have
   just sent to the Shopping List. A recipe category's title row shows how many
   of its recipes are bookmarked. Bookmarked recipes read at a glance as a
   light-blue pill.
9. Select several items and move them together: a "Select: off / on" toggle in
   its own row under the page title turns on a tap-to-select circle on every
   item badge. Move the whole selection either by dragging one selected badge,
   or — with no dragging at all, so it works on a phone — by pressing the green
   ⬇ "Move selected here" arrow that appears on each Kitchen pane, Longevity
   category, and Shopping List category while items are selected; the items stay
   selected so you can send them somewhere else too. While on, the toggle counts
   the current selection — "Select: on (5)". A "Deselect all" button appears
   beside the toggle once more than one item is picked. Badges still drag
   individually with a click-and-hold.
10. Organize the Shopping List into your own categories: it starts with a
    default set (Produce, Canned & Dry Goods, Dairy, and so on), and you can add
    your own, drag to-buy items into any of them, and delete a category — with a
    confirmation — from the ✕ on its title row.  Each category collapses to a
    labelled row with a count, matching the Recipes column.  Recipe and staple
    additions still land in the main list, ready to sort.  (The automatic
    grocery-department grouping is replaced by these categories.)
11. Delete a Longevity Foods category with the ✕ on its title row, with a
    confirmation.
12. Add your own food category to any tier of the Longevity Foods pyramid, then
    fill it with foods like the built-in categories.
13. Generate recipes with AI: an "AI" button in each recipe category opens an
    assistant that creates a recipe from the meal category, your remembered
    preferences and allergies, and — if you like — what is in your Kitchen; you
    preview it before it is added, and its missing ingredients can go straight
    to the Shopping List.  You bring your own LLM key (Google Gemini's free
    tier, OpenAI/ChatGPT, or Anthropic Claude); the request goes from your
    browser directly to the provider and your key is kept only in your browser.
14. Sign in and sign out through an OIDC identity provider, with each account
    keeping its own kitchen.
15. Add and remove Kitchen storage panes.
16. Edit a Kitchen pane's name and description inline — a pencil opens the
    editor, Enter saves, and Escape cancels without saving.
17. A Staples Tracker pane in the Kitchen: drag in the foods you always want on
    hand, see the ones you are out of turn red, and send those to the Shopping
    List with one button.
18. Move a recipe to another category by dragging it there; categories highlight
    as you drag over them.
19. Seed a new account with the default storage panes on its first sign-in.
20. =just login-demo= runs the app against a local identity provider for trying
    the signed-in experience.
21. Rename the app to Longevity Pantry (the code keeps the =food4u= name).
22. Recolour a Kitchen pane's header from the app's palette while editing it,
    and delete a pane — with a confirmation — from the same editor.
** Fixes
1. A recipe's cart button is now green only when the recipe has an ingredient
   that is neither in the Kitchen nor already on the Shopping List; when there
   is nothing left to add it is greyed out, so a glance says which recipes still
   need shopping.
2. The "Paste" and "AI" buttons in a recipe category now work: pressing one no
   longer dismisses the add row before the click takes effect.
3. Adding items after a fresh page load — by dragging, by the Staples Tracker
   cart button, and so on — no longer occasionally fails to save; newly created
   items are given ids that cannot collide with existing ones.
4. The Staples Tracker now starts collapsed, and its shopping-cart button stands
   out clearly against the header — and turns green when it holds staples not
   yet in the Kitchen or on the Shopping List.
5. Editing a Kitchen pane no longer clips the controls on the right edge, and
   clicking away from the pane closes the editor without saving.
